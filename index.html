Drona Capital is a private fund focusing on 'long only' equity investing in publicly listed small /mid cap companies by following time tested value investing principles. The firm seeks to look for exceptional businesses that are undervalued and invest in them long-term.

We value our customers’ needs of wealth preservation and therefore our objective is to ‘provide sustainable and adequate return on investment at minimal risk’.

Investment Approach

Invest in Attractive Businesses:

We take a fundamental approach of qualifying exceptional business based on fundamental analysis. Thorough research is carried out using various sources like the company annual reports, 10-k, 10-Q filing, company presentations and industry research.

Look for companies which have had strong historical financial performance in key return and liquidity ratios like return on invested capital and free cash flow.

This involves detailed study of the industry, the company and its competition, employing the Porter 5-Forces Framework to assess the industry attractiveness.

Buy at deep discounts

Invest only when the price of the stock is significantly lower than the intrinsic value of the business. If the business is attractive enough, we invest only if there is enough margin of safety or wait for the right moment to get a bargain price, when market inefficiency prices them below their real value.

Minimum Risk

Sustainable return is possible only if the risk is reduced. Thorough assessment is done of various risks-business risk (for example supplier or customer concentration), liquidity risk ( for example debt/equity ratio) and operational risk (for example inventory management ). Investment in a company is made only when these risk are significantly low.

Long term investments

Market price of a great business with consistent earning is likely to reflect the earning over long period. Therefore if we find a good business, we stay invested long term.Few companies in which we have long-term investments. Value is in years.

Investment Thesis

Take advantage of market inefficiencies from time to time

We have a ‘Contrarian’ approach and bad news might be good news for us. The market at times over reacts to a ‘bad news’.

A company might have had a bad quarter or gone through restructuring or recently announced a spin-off and market might be too pessimistic about it. This might lead to a low valuation for an otherwise excellent business, and this would be the right time for us to invest in the business.

During the financial meltdown, many exceptional businesses were available at a very cheap discount. Political changes or natural disasters have a similar effect, when markets could over react and great businesses available at a bargain. We are always looking for these opportunities.

Focus on small and mid-cap companies

The companies with Market Cap of less that US $10 Billion have higher probability of being neglected or escaping the radar of big mutual funds.

They are more likely to be unattractive or unexciting business for bigger funds and therefore more likely to be priced below their intrinsic value.

The median of our portfolio based on market cap is $7Bn

Excerpts from the recent Newsletter

When one hears the word innovation, generally a fast paced technology oriented company comes to mind, especially today as several new startups continue to emerge, launching a new mobile app, a new technology or a new way of doing business. Even the existing technology and internet companies are continuously innovating or acquiring companies which seem to have something new or different to offer to the customers. Innovation seems to be a mantra for success

We, on the other hand, have invested in companies which have a history of decades, and some of over a century of existence, and which at the outset seem to be doing same things for decades. So how have they survived without innovation? However, on the contrary, the fact is that every successful company which has managed to remain profitable and a leader in its industry has done so with smart innovations over the years

I would like to split innovations into two- the first type involves the introduction of a completely new idea or product which displaces the previous product / service / way of living – for instance, the move from horse carts to cars. In our portfolio, a couple of firms have innovated entirely new products, such as Cummins and Mettler Toledo

The second type of innovation involves the ongoing improvement to an initial idea in order to keep relevant and ahead of the times. This form of more regular innovation is, I believe, vital for the long-term growth and survival rate of any company, including almost all those in our portfolio as well as global century old businesses and those in the S&P 500.
